Introduction

A metric space $X$ is said to be complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to an element of $X$.

Let $V$ be a (complex) vector space. Then, a map $\norm{\cdot}:V\to[0,\infty)$ is called a norm on $V$ if the following conditions are satisfied:

  1. (Absolute homogeneity) For all $v\in V$, $\norm{\alpha v}=\lvert\alpha\rvert\norm{v}$.
  2. (Triangle inequality) For all $u,v\in V$, $\norm{u+v}\leq\norm{u} + \norm{v}$.
  3. (Positive-definite) $\norm{v}\geq0$ for all $v\in V$ and the equality holds if and only if $v=0$.
 A vector space equipped with a norm is called a normed vector space. Any normed vector space is a metric space with the metric $d(u,v)=\norm{u-v}$.

Let $V$ be a (complex) vector space. A map $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}:V\times V\to\mathbb{C}$ is an inner product on $V$ if:

  1. (Hermitian symmetry) $\inner{u}{v}=\overline{\inner{v}{u}}$ for all $u,v\in V$.
  2. (Sesquilinearity) $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}:V\times V\to\mathbb{C}$ is linear in the first argument and conjugate-linear in the second, i.e. for all $u,v,w\in V$ and $\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{C}$, $$\begin{aligned} \inner{\alpha u+\beta v}{w}&=\alpha\inner{u}{w}+\beta\inner{v}{w},\\ \inner{u}{\alpha v+\beta w}&=\overline\alpha\inner{u}{v}+\overline\beta\inner{u}{w}. \end{aligned}$$
  3. (Positive definiteness) $\inner{v}{v}\ge 0$ for all $v\in V$, and $\inner{v}{v}=0$ if and only if $v=0$.
 A vector space equipped with an inner product is called an inner product space.

Let $(V,\inner{\cdot}{\cdot})$ be an inner product space. The induced norm $\norm{\cdot}_{\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}}$ is $$\norm{\psi}_{\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}} = \sqrt{\inner{\psi}{\psi}}$$ where $\psi\in V$.

Hereafter, the subscript for the induced norm may be omitted.
Any inner product space also forms a normed vector space with respect to the induced norm by its inner product.

Let $\left(V, \inner{\cdot}{\cdot}\right)$ be an inner product space. Then for all $u,v\in V$, the following inequality holds: $$\left\lvert\inner{u}{v}\right\rvert\leq\norm{u}\cdot\norm{v}$$ where $\norm{\cdot}$ is the induced norm by the inner product $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}$.

Moreover, the equality holds if and only if $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent.

If $u=0$ or $v=0$ then claim is trivial. Assume $u,v\neq0$ and set $f(\alpha)=\norm{u-\alpha v}^2$.

By sesquilinearity and Hermitian symmetry of the inner product, $$\begin{aligned} 0\leq f(\alpha) &=\norm{u-\alpha v}^2 = \inner{u-\alpha v}{u-\alpha v} \\ &= \inner{u}{u-\alpha v}-\alpha\inner{v}{u-\alpha v} = \inner{u}{u} - \overline\alpha\inner{u}{v} - \alpha\inner{v}{u} + \alpha\overline\alpha\inner{v}{v} \\ &= \norm{u}^2 - \alpha\overline{\inner{u}{v}} - \overline\alpha\inner{u}{v} + \lvert\alpha\rvert^2\norm{v}^2. \end{aligned}$$ Taking $\alpha=\inner{u}{v}/\norm{v}^2$ gives $$\begin{aligned} f\left(\frac{\inner{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}\right) &= \norm{u}^2 - \frac{\inner{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}\overline{\inner{u}{v}} - \overline{\frac{\inner{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}}\inner{u}{v} + \left\lvert\frac{\inner{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}\right\rvert^2\norm{v}^2 \\ &= \norm{u}^2 - \frac{\lvert\inner{u}{v}\rvert^2}{\norm{v}^2} - \frac{\lvert\inner{u}{v}\rvert^2}{\norm{v}^2} + \frac{\lvert\inner{u}{v}\rvert^2}{\norm{v}^4}\norm{v}^2 \\ &= \norm{u}^2 - \frac{\lvert\inner{u}{v}\rvert^2}{\norm{v}^2} \geq 0, \end{aligned}$$ hence $\left\lvert\inner{u}{v}\right\rvert\leq\norm{u}\cdot\norm{v}$ holds.

Equality holds if and only if $u-\alpha v=0$ with $\alpha=\inner{u}{v}/\norm{v}^2$, i.e., $$u-\frac{\inner{u}{v}}{\norm{v}^2}v=0,$$ so $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent.

Banach Spaces

Every Hilbert space is a Banach space, but the converse is not true. A normed vector space $(V, \norm{\cdot})$ is called a Banach space if it is complete with respect to the norm $\norm{\cdot}$. Let $V$ and $W$ be normed vector spaces. A linear map (operator) $A:V\to W$ is bounded if $$\sup_{\substack{f\in V \\ f\neq 0}}\frac{\norm{Af}_W}{\norm{f}_V}=\sup_{\norm{f}_V=1}\norm{Af}_W<\infty.$$ The operator norm of a bounded operator $A:V\to W$ is $$\norm{A}=\sup_{\norm{f}_V=1}\norm{Af}_W.$$
Let $V=W$ and define a map $\mathrm{id}_W:W\rightarrow W$ by $f\mapsto f$. Then $$ \norm{\mathrm{id}_W} =\sup_{\norm{f}_W=1}\norm{\mathrm{id}_W f}_W =\sup_{\norm{f}_W=1}\norm{f}_W=1, $$ so $\mathrm{id}_W$ is bounded with $\norm{\mathrm{id}_W}=1$.
Counterexample.

Let $W=C^0([0,1])=\{f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{C}\mid f\mathrm{\ is\ continuous}\}$. Define a norm by $\norm{f}_\infty=\sup_{x\in[0,1]}\lvert f(x)\rvert$. (It is called the maximum norm.) Then we can easily show that $(W,\norm{\cdot}_\infty)$ is complete, so is a Banach space.

Now define $P:C^1([0,1])\to C^0([0,1])$ by $f\mapsto f'$. Clearly, $P$ is linear.

Note that $C^1([0,1])\subseteq C^0([0,1])$, so we can inherit a norm $\norm{\cdot}_\infty$ from $C^0([0,1])$ to $C^1([0,1])$. We can easily show that $(C^1([0,1]),\norm{\cdot}_\infty)$ is not a Banach space, but still is a normed vector space.

We claim that $P$ is not bounded.

Consider the sequence $f_n(x)=\sin(2\pi nx)$ in $C^1([0,1])$ for $n\in\mathbb{N}$. Then we have $\norm{f_n}_\infty=1$. Since $$(Pf_n)(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\sin(2\pi nx)=2\pi n\cos(2\pi nx),$$ we have $\norm{Pf_n}_\infty=2\pi n$.

Thus, $$\norm{P}=\sup_{\norm{f}_\infty=1}\norm{Pf}_\infty\geq\sup_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\norm{Pf_n}_\infty=\sup_{n\in\mathbb{N}}2\pi n=\infty,$$ therefore, $P$ is not bounded.

Although in quantum mechanics we do not use the maximum norm $\lVert\cdot\rVert_\infty$ from the above example, and the momentum operator is defined differently — in one spatial dimension as $$P(\psi) = -\,i\,\hbar\,\psi'(x),$$ where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant — the example still suggests that the momentum operator is not bounded.

The same conclusion applies to the position operator as well.

Let $V$ be a normed vector space and $W$ be a Banach space. Then the set $$\mathcal{L}(V,W)=\{A:V\xrightarrow{\ \textrm{linear}\ }W\mid\norm{A}<\infty\}$$ is a Banach space if equipped with the operator norm $\norm{\cdot}_{\mathcal{L}(V,W)}$.

Proof. (completeness)

Assume that $\{A_n\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a Cauchy sequence in $\mathcal{L}(V,W)$. Then for a given $\varepsilon>0$, there exists $N\in\mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n,m\ge N$, $$\norm{A_n - A_m} < \varepsilon.$$

  1. (Construction)

    For each $f\in V$, we have $$ \norm{A_nf-A_mf}_W=\norm{(A_n-A_m)f}_W\leq\norm{A_n-A_m}\cdot\norm{f}_V <\varepsilon\cdot\norm{f}_V $$ and $\norm{f}_V$ is finite, $\{A_nf\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\subseteq W$ is also a Cauchy sequence.

    Since $(W,\norm{\cdot}_W)$ is a Banach space, the limit $\lim_{n\to\infty}A_nf$ exists in $W$.

    Now, define a map $A:V\to W$ by $f\mapsto\lim_{n\to\infty}A_nf$.

  2. (Linearity)

    For all $f,g\in V$ and for all $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}$, $$\begin{aligned} A(f+g)&=\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n(f+g) =\lim_{n\to\infty}(A_nf+A_ng) \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty}A_nf+\lim_{n\to\infty}A_ng =Af+Ag, \end{aligned}$$ and similarly $A(\lambda f)=\lambda\cdot Af$.

  3. (Boundedness)

    Fix $m = N$. By the triangle inequality, $$\norm{A_n} \le \norm{A_n - A_N} + \norm{A_N} < \varepsilon + \norm{A_N}.$$ Hence $\{\norm{A_n}\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is bounded by $$M = \max\left\{\norm{A_1},\dots,\norm{A_{N-1}},\,\varepsilon + \norm{A_N}\right\} < \infty.$$

    For each $f \in V$, by continuity of the norm, $$\norm{A f}_W = \norm{\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n f}_W = \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{A_n f}_W \le \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{A_n}\norm{f}_V \le M\norm{f}_V.$$ Therefore $A$ is bounded and $\norm{A} \le M$.

  4. (Convergence)

    For all non-zero $f\in V$, since $$ \frac{\norm{(A_n-A_m)f}_W}{\norm{f}_V} \leq\sup_{\substack{f\in V \\ f\neq 0}}\frac{\norm{(A_n-A_m)f}_W}{\norm{f}_V} =\norm{A_n-A_m}<\varepsilon, $$ we have $$\norm{(A_n-A_m)f}_W<\varepsilon\cdot\norm{f}_V.$$

    It follows that $$\lim_{m\to\infty}\norm{A_nf-A_mf}_W\leq\varepsilon\cdot\norm{f}_V,$$ and since the norm $\norm{\cdot}_W$ is continuous, $$ \lim_{m\to\infty}\norm{A_nf-A_mf}_W=\norm{A_nf-\lim_{m\to\infty}A_mf}_W=\norm{A_nf-Af}_W\leq\varepsilon\cdot\norm{f}_V, $$ which follows that $$ \norm{A_n-A}=\sup_{\substack{f\in V \\ f\neq0}}\frac{\norm{(A_n-A)f}_W}{\norm{f}_V}\leq\varepsilon. $$

    Thus, $A_n$ converges to $A$.

Note that $A\in\mathcal{L}(V,W)$ since $A$ is linear and bounded. Therefore, any Cauchy sequence in $\mathcal{L}(V,W)$ converges in $\mathcal{L}(V,W)$, so $\mathcal{L}(V,W)$ is complete.
Note that $(\mathcal{L}(V,W), \norm{\cdot})$ is Banach, even if $V$ is only normed.

An important insight about the domain of a bounded linear map is the BLT theorem (bounded linear transformation theorem).

BLT stands for ‘Bounded Linear Transformation’.

Let $V$ be a normed vector space and $W$ be a Banach space. Consider a linear map $A:\mathcal{D}_A\to W$ that is bounded with respect to the subspace norm on $\mathcal{D}_A$, where $\mathcal{D}_A\subseteq V$ is dense ($\overline{\mathcal{D}_A}=V$).

Then there is a unique extension of $A$ to a bounded map $\hat{A}\in\mathcal{L}(V,W)$, which satisfies, of course, $\hat{A}|_{\mathcal{D}_A}=A$.

In other words, we may (and always will) extend any bounded operator to the entire normed space at hand. But, unbounded operators may not.

Let $A\in\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{D}_A,W)$.

  1. (Construction)

    Let $f\in V$ and consider a sequence $\{ f_n\}\subseteq\mathcal{D}_A$ with $\lim_{n\to\infty}f_n=f$. Then $\{f_n\}$ is certainly a Cauchy sequence. Note that $\{Af_n\}$ is again a Cauchy sequence in $W$. Since $W$ is Banach, $$\lim_{n\to\infty}Af_n\in W.$$

    Define the extension $\hat{A}:V\to W$ by $$f\mapsto \hat{A}f=\lim_{n\to\infty}Af_n.$$

  2. (Well-defined)

    This definition is well-defined, i.e. does not depend on the choice of $\{f_n\}$. Moreover, we can always construct such $\{f_n\}$ since $\mathcal{D}_A$ is dense in $V$.

  3. (Linearity)

    For $f_n\to f$ and $g_n\to g$, $$ \hat{A}f+\hat{A}g=\lim_{n\to\infty}Af_n+\lim_{n\to\infty}Ag_n = \lim_{n\to\infty}A(f_n+g_n)=\hat{A}(f+g), $$ since $(f_n+g_n)\to(f+g)$.

  4. (Boundedness)

    $\hat A$ is bounded and that, indeed $\norm{\hat A}=\norm{A}$.

Let $(V,\norm{\cdot}_V)$ be a normed vector space. The (topological) dual space of $V$ is defined by $$ \left(V^*,\norm{\cdot}\right)=\left(\mathcal{L}(V,\mathbb{C}),\norm{\cdot}_{\mathcal{L}(V,\mathbb{C})}\right). $$

See for the definition of $\mathcal{L}(V,\mathbb{C})$.
 Let $(V,\norm{\cdot}_V)$ be a normed vector space. Then for all linear functionals $l:V\to\mathbb{C}$, $l$ is continuous if and only if $l$ is bounded.
In finite-dimensional spaces, every linear functional is automatically continuous, so the algebraic and (topological) duals coincide. In infinite-dimensional normed spaces, the (topological) dual $V^*$ denotes the space of continuous (i.e. bounded) linear functionals $V\to\mathbb{C}$.
Let $V$ be a normed vector space. A sequence $\{f_n\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\subseteq V$ is said to

  1. converge strongly to $f\in V$ if

    $$\forall\varepsilon>0,\ \exists N\in\mathbb{N},\ \forall n\geq N,\ \norm{f-f_n}<\varepsilon.$$

  2. converge weakly to $f\in V$ if

    $$\forall l\in V^*,\ l(f_n)\to l(f)\textrm{ in }\mathbb{C}.$$

Strong convergence implies weak convergence.

For all $l\in V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,\mathbb{C})$, $l$ is bounded and hence continuous by . Thus $f_n\to f$ implies $l(f_n)\to l(f)$.

Separable Hilbert Spaces

Hilbert Spaces

A (complex) inner product space $(\hilbert,\inner{\cdot}{\cdot})$ is called a (complex) Hilbert space if the following conditions are satisfied:

  1. $\hilbert$ is equipped with an inner product $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}$.
  2. The inner product induces a norm $\norm{\cdot}$ on $\hilbert$.
  3. $\hilbert$ is complete with respect to this norm.
The norm $\norm{\cdot}:\hilbert\to\mathbb{R}$ of the Hilbert space $\hilbert$, which is defined by $\psi\mapsto\sqrt{\inner{\psi}{\psi}}$, makes $(\hilbert,\norm{\cdot})$ into a Banach space.

Let $\hilbert$ be a Hilbert space. Then

  1. $\left(\mathcal{L}(\hilbert,\hilbert),\norm{\cdot}_{\mathcal{L}(\hilbert,\hilbert)}\right)$ is a Banach space.
  2. $\left(\hilbert^*,\norm{\cdot}_{\hilbert^*}\right)=\left(\mathcal{L}(\hilbert,\mathbb{C}),\norm{\cdot}_{\mathcal{L}(\hilbert,\mathbb{C})}\right)$ is a Banach space.
. Trivial.
We will see that $\hilbert^*$ is even a Hilbert space.

Let $(V, \norm{\cdot})$ be a normed vector space. Then there exists an inner product $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}$ on $V$ which induces the norm $\norm{\cdot}$ if and only if the parallelogram identity holds for all $f,g\in V$: $$\norm{f+g}^2+\norm{f-g}^2=2\norm{f}^2+2\norm{g}^2.$$

For $(\Leftarrow)$, a typical inner product is given by the polarization identity: $$\inner{f}{g}=\frac{1}{4}\left(\norm{f+g}^2-\norm{f-g}^2+i\norm{f-ig}^2-\norm{f+ig}^2\right).$$
Let $\hilbert$ be a Hilbert space. Then, there exists an inner product $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}_{\hilbert^*}$ in $\hilbert^*$ which makes $\left(\hilbert^*,\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}_{\hilbert^*}\right)$ into a Hilbert space.
This theorem can be proved after introducing the Riesz Representation Theorem.

Hamel & Schauder bases

The notion of basis for a vector space $V$ is known from linear algebra:

Let $V$ be a vector space. Then a subset $\mathcal{B}\subseteq V$ is called a (Hamel) basis if:

  1. $\mathcal{B}$ is linearly independent, i.e. for any distinct $e_1,\dots,e_n\in\mathcal{B}$ and $v^1,\dots,v^n\in\mathbb{C}$, $$ \sum_{i=1}^n v^i e_i = 0 \quad\implies\quad v^1 = \dots = v^n = 0. $$
  2. for every $v\in V$, there exists a finite subset $\{e_1,\cdots,e_n\}\subseteq\mathcal{B}$ and $v^1,\cdots,v^n\in\mathbb{C}$ such that $$v=\sum_{i=1}^nv^ie_i.$$
Let $V$ be a vector space. Then, any two bases have the same cardinality.

Let $V$ be a vector space. Then the dimension of $V$ is denoted by $\dim V$, and is defined by:

  1. If there exists a finite basis $\mathcal{B}$, then $\dim V=\lvert\mathcal{B}\rvert$.
  2. Otherwise, $\dim V=\infty$.
This definition is well-defined by .
 However, this is not the notion of basis that is used in quantum mechanics. For in fact, on a Hilbert space $\hilbert$, one may define a refined notion of basis:

Let $V$ be a normed vector space. Then a sequence $\mathcal{S} = \{e_n\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\subseteq V$ is called a (Schauder) basis if:

  1. $\mathcal{S}$ is linearly independent, i.e. every finite subset of $\mathcal{S}$ is linearly independent.
  2. for every $v\in V$, there exists a unique sequence $\{v^i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}\subseteq\mathbb{C}$ such that $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{v - \sum_{i=1}^n v^i e_i} = 0. $$
